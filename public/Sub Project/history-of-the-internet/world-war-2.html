The ability to transmit messages within the military and the allies in complete secrecy is one of the most important aspects ofcommunications during warfare. Today, with all of the technological advancement that make high- technology digital encryption systems available, ensuring security and privacy when transmitting codes of message is fairly easy. However, during the World War II, the need for confidentiality in message transmission led those involved to develop their own various forms of encrypted communication. These methods ranged from sending trained carrier pigeons to developing new electronic encryption systems. Various countries involved in World War II all fought the same battle for secrecy, so when the Germans developed their own system called the Enigma, they thought they had the advantage among the rest. Enigma was very much like a typewriter except for its ability to encode highly encrypted text messages. It was originally intended for commercial purposes, but then the German military saw its potential. The German military seemed like an impenetrable fortress because of their remarkable encryption system, but after the Allies in Poland obtained an Enigma machine, they were joined by British and French forces in high hopes of decrypting the German codes. The effort of setting up a school in Buckinghamshire's Bletchley Park and bringing in mathematicians, logicians, and experts in problem solving bore fruit when these experts created prototypes of electronic machines to decrypt codes on a much larger scale. By this time, the German army was still confident with the secrecy they enjoy using the Enigma, so much so that they adopted the Enigma ciphers within all of their army, navy, air force, and secret services. Earlier attempts in cracking the Enigma codes only revealed bits of information, but in 1941 new information was discovered that revealed Germany's plans to invade Greece. Not long after, other intel was decrypted and revealed information on the Italian navy, which resulted to the Allies' victory during the Battle of Cape Matapan.
